Life is plagued with the incongruency of our inputs and outputs. It's like in the TV show [Severance](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt11280740/). Your hobbies, what you read, do, watch, listen to—your life—your inputs have no impact from your work, or your outputs. This divorce between our two lives lead to an unresolved tension. A lack of purpose, of direction.

This came to me from[ Gwern's interview on Dwarkesh's podcast](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=a42key59cZQ). From his research method—rabbit holes, looking for things that spark.

> a lot of thinkers have tackled this, though not always as directly as severance frames it. here are some you might dig:
> 
> 1. **marx** - _alienation of labor_: workers become estranged from their own humanity when the product of their labor, the process of work, and even their sense of self is owned and controlled by someone else. pretty foundational for understanding the input/output split.
>     
> 2. **simone weil** - _gravity and grace_: she digs into how modern labor strips people of spiritual meaning. she wasn’t a marxist but had overlapping ideas about how work and meaning diverge.
>     
> 3. **max weber** - _the protestant ethic and the spirit of capitalism_: charts how capitalism fused with this weird protestant work-ethic narrative, creating an obligation to work for work’s sake, even when the output lacks meaning.
>     
> 4. **guy debord** - _society of the spectacle_: not directly about work but about how modern life turns us into passive consumers of images and experiences rather than active participants. our "inputs" (media, entertainment, hobbies) are commodified, so even they become alienated.
>     
> 5. **han byung-chul** - _the burnout society_: he writes about how modern productivity culture turns everyone into "self-exploiters," where we collapse work and life but still feel purposeless because it’s all about output without deeper meaning.
>     
> 6. **david graeber** - _bullshit jobs_: argues that many modern jobs are meaningless, creating existential dread because people know their work doesn’t matter but they’re trapped in the system anyway.
>     
> 7. **hannah arendt** - _the human condition_: breaks life into labor, work, and action, arguing that labor (survival work) has consumed too much of modern life, leaving no room for meaningful action or thought.
>     
> 
> these all circle around the same fundamental question: how do we reconcile being human with living in systems that treat us as tools?
